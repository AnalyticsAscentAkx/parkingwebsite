Enforcement

How Dutch parking enforcement works in 2026

🚗

Scan Cars (Scanautos)

Automated vehicles with roof-mounted cameras scan every number plate and cross-reference instantly with paid parking databases. In Amsterdam, a scan car passes every 20–30 minutes. There is no grace period — the fine is issued the moment your paid time expires.

👮

Parking Wardens (BOA)

Buitengewoon opsporingsambtenaar (BOA) wardens patrol independently on foot or bicycle. They issue fines manually and can call for towing. BOAs have full enforcement authority and work alongside the scan car system.

📡

ANPR Technology

Automatic Number Plate Recognition (ANPR) cameras are also fixed at garage entrances and some street locations. Your plate is matched against permit databases in real time. Foreign plates are checked against the European EUCARIS system.

What happens after you get a Dutch parking fine

When you return to your car, you'll find a yellow or white envelope (envelope) under the wiper or attached to the car. This is the naheffingsaanslag — the parking fine notice. It shows the fine amount, the date, time, location, and your fine reference number (kenmerk).

Payment timeline and escalation

You have 6 weeks (42 days) from the date on the fine to either pay or appeal. Here's exactly what happens if you don't:

  • Week 1–6: Pay €72.90 (+ unpaid fee). No surcharge.
  • After 6 weeks: A €9 administrative surcharge (aanmaning) is added.
  • After 8 weeks: File passes to the CJIB (Centraal Justitieel Incassobureau) — the national collection agency.
  • CJIB collection: Additional costs added. Dutch residents risk vehicle registration block. Foreign plates enter EU debt collection.
  • Worst case: Bailiff involvement, costs can exceed €200+ on a €72.90 original fine.

Foreign plates — do Dutch fines follow you home?

Yes. This is one of the most common tourist misconceptions. Many visitors assume that because they're driving a German, Belgian, French, or British-registered vehicle, the Dutch authorities can't reach them. This is incorrect.

The Netherlands is part of the EUCARIS system (European Car and Driving Licence Information System), which allows Dutch municipalities to look up the registered owner of any EU vehicle plate. The fine is then sent by post to the owner's home address abroad.

For countries with bilateral debt collection agreements (Germany, Belgium, France, Luxembourg, Austria, Switzerland, UK), unpaid Dutch fines can be collected by enforcement authorities in your home country. Ignoring a Dutch fine is rarely cost-free.

How to appeal a Dutch parking fine — step by step

You have 6 weeks from the fine date to submit an objection (bezwaar). Here's the process:

Step 1 — Check your grounds

Valid grounds for a successful appeal include: the parking meter or machine was broken, signage at the location was missing or unclear, you had a valid permit that the scanner couldn't read, there was a genuine administrative error on the fine, or the scan car recorded incorrect data. "I didn't see the sign" and "I was only a few minutes" are not valid grounds.

Step 2 — Gather evidence

Take photos of the parking location, any signs, the meter, and your payment receipt if applicable. The more specific your evidence, the stronger your appeal.

Step 3 — Submit online or by post

The fastest way is to appeal online at CJIB.nl using your fine reference number (kenmerk). You can write in English — the form accepts submissions in multiple languages. Alternatively, write a letter to the municipality named on the fine notice (gemeente) and send it by recorded post.

Step 4 — Wait for the decision

Municipalities typically respond within 6–12 weeks. During this time, you don't need to pay the fine (the appeal process pauses the deadline). If your appeal is rejected, you have a further right to appeal to the administrative court (bestuursrechter).

What actually gets upheld?

Appeals succeed in roughly 15–25% of cases. The most commonly upheld grounds are: broken payment infrastructure (the machine wasn't working), incorrectly positioned signs, and scan car technical errors. In Amsterdam, the municipality publishes its appeal decision percentages annually — in 2025 roughly 18% of appeals were upheld.

Blue zone parking rules explained

Blue zones (blauwe zones) are free parking areas where you must display a parking disc (parkeerschijf) — a physical cardboard or plastic clock placed behind the windscreen, visible from the front.

How to use a parking disc

Set the disc to your arrival time, rounded up to the next half hour. So if you arrive at 10:07, set it to 10:30. The maximum stay is shown on the blue zone sign — usually 1 or 2 hours. When you return, move your car or rotate the disc. You cannot use a parking app in a blue zone — it must be the physical disc.

Where to get a parking disc

Parking discs (parkeerschijven) are free and available at: ANWB shops and service points, most petrol stations (tankstations), some supermarkets and bookshops, and at many tourist information offices. They cost nothing and last indefinitely.

Blue zone fine

Parking in a blue zone without displaying a disc, or having exceeded the maximum time, results in the standard €72.90 fine. There is no warning system — it's issued immediately on detection.

Resident permit zones — what tourists need to know

Large areas of Dutch cities are reserved for residents with a parkeervergunning (parking permit). These zones are marked with a blue bordered sign showing a permit zone code (like "E3" or "Centrum A"). Even if you pay at the parking machine, if the zone requires a resident permit, the payment is for visitors' spaces only — and those may be limited or non-existent in that block.

In Amsterdam, approximately 60% of street parking spaces are residents-only. Always check that the space you're parking in is not marked with a permit zone sign that excludes visitors.
